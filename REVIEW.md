# Textbook Review: The American Economy: A Structural Geography

**Reviewer**: Claude (AI assistant)
**Date**: February 2026
**Scope**: Full manuscript review (30 chapters, 3 appendices, interactive visualizations)

---

## Overall Score: 4 out of 5

This is a strong, well-organized textbook that fills a genuine gap in the educational landscape. There is no widely available book that provides this kind of institutional, structural map of the American economy — sector by sector, region by region, with concrete data on who the major players are, how money flows, and how the pieces connect. The writing is clear and engaging, the data work is solid, and the consistent chapter structure makes it highly usable as both a learning tool and a reference. The main limitations are several missing sectors, light treatment of inequality and environmental economics, and the absence of exercises for classroom use.

---

## Strengths

### 1. Fills a Real Gap
The book's premise — that neither economics courses nor business schools provide a systematic map of the economic terrain — is correct. Standard intro economics teaches supply-and-demand models; this book teaches you that 0.3% of firms employ 54% of workers, that healthcare spending has grown from 5% to 18% of GDP since 1960, and that the three largest PBMs control 80% of the drug pricing market. This institutional detail is genuinely hard to find assembled in one place.

### 2. Consistent, Well-Designed Chapter Structure
The sector chapters in Part II follow a disciplined template (overview, how the industry works, industry structure, geographic distribution, workforce, regulation, lobbying, trends, firm profiles, data sources). This makes it easy to compare sectors and use the book as a reference. Readers can quickly find, for example, the top firms in any industry or how a sector is regulated.

### 3. Excellent Writing Quality
The prose is clear, confident, and free of unnecessary jargon. Opening hooks are effective — the MRI price comparison that opens the healthcare chapter, the paradox of rising output and falling employment in manufacturing, the hierarchy of money in finance. The book reads well as narrative, not just as reference material.

### 4. Strong Data Foundations
The book draws on official government sources (BEA, BLS, Census, Federal Reserve) and cites them consistently. Tables are well-constructed and informative. The appendices on data sources, BEA industry accounts, and NAICS codes add genuine practical value for readers who want to do their own analysis.

### 5. Systems Thinking and Interconnections
The book does not treat sectors as isolated silos. Chapter 2 (input-output framework), the value chain examples, and Chapter 30 (shock transmission) all emphasize interdependence. The oil price collapse case study traced through multiple chapters shows how a single shock propagates through supply chains, regional economies, financial markets, and labor markets. This is the book's most distinctive intellectual contribution.

### 6. Effective Pedagogical Features
- **Hint boxes** for key concepts, common misconceptions, and deep dives (e.g., the Baumol effect, the money multiplier correction)
- **Key takeaways** at the end of each chapter
- **Firm profiles** that make abstract industry structures concrete
- **Further reading** with well-chosen academic and popular references
- **Interactive visualizations** (GDP treemap, employment chart, circular flow, regional map, shock propagation, data explorer) that let readers explore the data

### 7. Intellectually Honest
The book does not shy away from complexity or contested claims. The healthcare chapter honestly describes the system's dysfunction. The manufacturing chapter presents the "China shock" research without either demonizing trade or dismissing the pain. The finance chapter corrects the textbook money multiplier story with the endogenous money view, citing the Bank of England. This intellectual honesty builds trust with the reader.

### 8. Strong Regional Analysis
Part V (Regional Economies) is a highlight. The Northeast Corridor chapter captures how four distinct metros function as a unified mega-region. The treatment of the Sunbelt's growth model, the Midwest's manufacturing transition, and rural America's challenges is nuanced and data-rich.

## Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

### 1. Missing Sectors (Major Gap)
Several economically significant sectors lack dedicated chapters:

- **Agriculture and Food Production**: Agriculture is discussed only within value chains and as an input to food manufacturing. Given its political significance, its role in rural economies (Chapter 25), and the complexity of agricultural policy (subsidies, crop insurance, trade), a dedicated chapter is warranted.
- **Leisure and Hospitality**: At 11% of employment, this is the fourth-largest employer in the economy. Hotels, restaurants, entertainment, and tourism have distinct business models, geographic patterns, and labor market dynamics that deserve treatment.
- **Mining and Utilities**: Mining gets brief mentions in the energy chapter, but utilities — electricity generation, water systems, broadband — are critical infrastructure that shapes every other sector.
- **Arts, Entertainment, and Recreation**: Hollywood, the music industry, professional sports, and the gaming industry are economically significant and culturally distinctive American industries.

**Suggestion**: Add at least agriculture and leisure/hospitality as standalone chapters. Utilities could be folded into the energy chapter with expanded scope.

### 2. Inequality Receives Insufficient Attention
The book mentions inequality in passing (the GDP-employment gap, wage divergence by sector) but never provides sustained treatment of:

- Income and wealth distribution trends (the top 1%, the declining labor share)
- Racial economic disparities (the Black-white wealth gap, occupational segregation)
- Gender pay gaps and occupational sorting
- Geographic inequality (divergence between thriving metros and declining communities)

These are among the most important structural features of the American economy. A chapter or extended section on "Who Benefits: Distribution and Inequality" would strengthen the book significantly.

**Suggestion**: Add a chapter in Part I or Part VII on the distributional structure of the economy, drawing on CBO, Federal Reserve SCF, and Census data.

### 3. Environmental and Climate Economics Are Underrepresented
Environmental costs and climate risk are mentioned briefly (GDP limitations, energy transition) but the book lacks systematic treatment of:

- The economic costs of climate change (physical risk, transition risk)
- Environmental regulation as an economic force (cap-and-trade, clean air/water)
- The green energy transition as a structural economic shift
- Natural resource depletion and sustainability

Given that the IRA is discussed extensively in the manufacturing chapter, a broader framework for understanding the economy-environment relationship would be natural.

**Suggestion**: Expand the energy chapter or add a dedicated section on environmental economics.

### 4. The Digital/Platform Economy Needs Deeper Treatment
Chapter 11 covers "Information and Technology" but doesn't deeply address:

- Platform economics (network effects, winner-take-all dynamics, multi-sided markets)
- The gig economy (Uber, DoorDash, Instacart) and its labor market implications
- Data as an economic resource and surveillance capitalism
- AI and its emerging economic effects

These are arguably the most important structural changes underway in the economy.

**Suggestion**: Expand Chapter 11 or split it into "Traditional Tech/Media" and "The Platform Economy."

### 5. No Exercises, Discussion Questions, or Problem Sets
For a textbook aimed partly at undergraduates, the absence of exercises limits classroom adoption. Each chapter could benefit from:

- Discussion questions that require applying concepts to current events
- Data exercises that point students to the cited sources (BEA, BLS, FRED) to find and interpret data themselves
- Short analytical exercises (e.g., "Using the input-output framework, trace how a 20% increase in oil prices would affect three industries discussed in Part II")

**Suggestion**: Add 5-8 discussion questions and 2-3 data exercises per chapter.

### 6. Minor Data Inconsistencies
A few statistics appear inconsistent across chapters:

- Chapter 1 states manufacturing is 11% of GDP; Chapter 9 says "approximately 10%." The discrepancy is small but should be reconciled.
- The GDP figures in Chapter 1's Table 1.1 sum to more than 100% due to rounding, which should be noted.
- Some chapters use 2022 data, others 2023, and a few reference 2024 figures. A clearer convention for the reference year would help.

**Suggestion**: Do a consistency pass across all chapters to align key statistics and note the reference year for each table.

### 7. Some Repetition Across Chapters
The 2014-2016 oil price collapse is covered in detail in Chapter 2, referenced in Chapter 14 (Energy), and analyzed again in Chapter 30. While some repetition is natural in a reference work, the overlap between Chapters 2 and 30 is substantial. Similarly, the China shock appears in both Chapter 9 and Chapter 30 with overlapping content.

**Suggestion**: Consolidate the detailed case studies in Chapter 30 and use shorter cross-references in earlier chapters, or differentiate the analysis more clearly (e.g., Chapter 2 covers the input-output mechanics, Chapter 30 covers the full multi-channel propagation).

### 8. International Comparisons Could Be More Systematic
The book occasionally compares the U.S. to other countries (healthcare costs, robot density, GDP rankings) but lacks a systematic comparative framework. Given the book's audience, a brief treatment of how the American economic model differs from European social market economies or East Asian developmental states would provide valuable context.

**Suggestion**: Expand Chapter 29 (The American Economy in Perspective) with more structured international comparison.

### 9. The Labor Chapter Could Be Strengthened
Chapter 28 on labor organizations is the narrowest chapter in the book. It could be expanded to cover:

- The changing nature of work (remote work, freelancing, noncompete agreements)
- Labor market monopsony and employer market power
- Immigration's role in the labor market
- The childcare and eldercare infrastructure that enables labor force participation

**Suggestion**: Rename to "Labor Markets and the Workforce" and expand scope beyond unions and trade associations.

### 10. Figures and Visualizations
The 125 PNG figures and 6 interactive visualizations are a strong feature. However:

- Some figures are referenced in the text but their content can't be verified in review (only filenames are visible)
- Alt text on figures is sometimes generic rather than descriptive
- Interactive visualizations could include guided exercises ("Try selecting the manufacturing sector and comparing its GDP share to its employment share")

**Suggestion**: Review all figure alt text for accessibility. Add guided prompts to interactive tools.

## Summary

**The American Economy: A Structural Geography** is an ambitious and largely successful attempt to map the American economy as it actually exists. It fills a genuine gap between theoretical economics textbooks and narrow industry reports. The writing is clear, the data is solid, and the emphasis on interconnection and systems thinking distinguishes it from more conventional treatments.

The main areas for improvement are: (1) adding missing sectors (agriculture, hospitality), (2) treating inequality and environmental economics more seriously, (3) adding exercises for classroom use, and (4) tightening some repetitive content across chapters.

As a reference for journalists, policy professionals, and educated general readers, it works well already. With the additions suggested above, it could become a standard undergraduate text in economic geography or institutional economics.
